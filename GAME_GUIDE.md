# Threadbare — Content Tracker & Guide

A living reference of everything in the game. Update this whenever new content is added.

---

## Stats

Health · Joy · Smarts · Looks · Athletic · Fame — each 0–100. Plus hidden meters: Stress, Social Followers. Money and Net Worth are tracked separately.

---

## Traits (rolled at birth, 2 per character)

Curious · Tough · Charming · Restless · Kind · Sly · Lucky · Driven. Traits nudge odds throughout life (e.g. Lucky improves gambling and windfalls; Driven speeds career training; Charming helps elections and dating).

---

## Education tiers

0 = No degree · 1 = Trade / Academy · 2 = Bachelor's · 3 = Doctorate. Careers gate on these.

## Careers

### Simple careers (flat pay that grows with years)
Cashier, Barista, Janitor, Truck Driver, Mechanic, Electrician, Plumber, Farmer, Accountant, Financial Analyst, Engineer, Teacher, Nurse, Veterinarian, Airline Pilot.

### Ladder careers (random promotions up a fixed track)
- **Police Officer:** Cadet → Officer → Detective → Sergeant → Chief
- **Firefighter:** Recruit → Firefighter → Lieutenant → Captain → Fire Chief
- **Military Officer:** Private → Sergeant → Lieutenant → Major → General
- **Corporate:** Intern → Associate → Manager → VP → CEO
- **Chef:** Line Cook → Chef → Sous Chef → Head Chef → Executive Chef
- **Lawyer:** Associate → Lawyer → Senior Partner → Judge
- **Surgeon:** Resident → Doctor → Surgeon → Chief of Surgery
- **Secret Agent:** Recruit → Field Agent → Senior Agent → Station Chief → Director
- **Politician:** Candidate → Councilor → Mayor → Governor → President (advances by winning elections; costs campaign money)

### Wild careers (boom-or-bust, fame-driven)
YouTuber, Musician, Actor, Model (needs looks 70+), Author, Real Estate Agent, Startup Founder, NBA Player (athletic 82+), Pro Footballer (athletic 80+), UFC Fighter (athletic 78+).

### Deep staged careers (climb named stages, gated by a key skill + years)
Each stage pays `base × multiplier`. You advance when you hit the skill requirement AND spend the minimum years. Training (in the Do menu) pushes the key stat up faster. Matching a high-school club gives a head start (skip to stage 2).

- **Pro Wrestler** (key: Athletic, gate 55) — feeder club: Wrestling Team
  Indie Circuit → NXT Developmental → SmackDown Roster → Raw Main Eventer → World Champion → Hall of Famer
- **Actor → Movie Star** (key: Looks) — feeder club: Drama Club
  Background Extra → Commercial Actor → TV Series Regular → Supporting Film → Leading Role → A-List Movie Star → Oscar Winner
- **Tech / IT** (key: Smarts, needs Trade/Academy) — feeder club: Coding Club
  Help Desk → IT Support → Junior Developer → Software Engineer → Senior Engineer → Engineering Lead → CTO
- **Boxer** (key: Athletic, gate 60) — feeder club: Boxing Club
  Amateur → Pro Debut → Ranked Contender → Title Challenger → World Champion
- **Film Director** (key: Smarts, needs Trade/Academy) — feeder club: Film Club
  Film School Grad → Music Video Dir. → Indie Director → Studio Director → Blockbuster Dir. → Legendary Auteur
- **Pop Star** (key: Fame) — feeder club: School Band
  Open Mic Singer → Local Gigging → Signed Artist → Charting Single → Platinum Album → Global Pop Icon
- **Chef → Restaurateur** (key: Smarts, needs Trade/Academy) — feeder club: Culinary Club
  Line Cook → Sous Chef → Head Chef → Restaurant Owner → Restaurant Group → Celebrity Chef
- **Esports Pro** (key: Smarts) — feeder club: Esports Club
  Ranked Grinder → Semi-Pro → Pro Team Sub → Starting Roster → World Finalist → Esports Legend
- **Astronaut** (key: Smarts, needs Doctorate) — feeder club: Science Olympiad
  Candidate → Mission Specialist → Shuttle Crew → Mission Commander → Moonwalker
- **Organized Crime** (no degree; rewards the Sly trait)
  Street Hustler → Enforcer → Crew Leader → Underboss → Kingpin
  Each stage pays far more but carries escalating **bust risk**. Getting caught means prison — sentences grow with how deep you were, and a Kingpin can catch a **life sentence**. In prison you serve time, may earn **parole** for good behavior, and a **case review / appeal** can surface (witness recants, mishandled evidence, DNA, police misconduct) and overturn the conviction — rare for lifers, so freedom is never guaranteed.
- **Superhero / Vigilante** (key: Athletic, gate 70) — fight crime, take injuries, earn from endorsements at higher stages, and trigger heroic world events.
  Masked Vigilante → Local Hero → City Guardian → National Icon → Legendary Hero
- **Supervillain** (key: Smarts; uses crime/prison mechanics but evades capture more easily) — heists fund you; world-threatening deeds leave a mark.
  Petty Menace → Costumed Criminal → Crime Syndicate → Arch-Nemesis → World Threat
  You can also become a villain **without** the crime career — a "turning point" event can offer the dark path after hardship.

### Powers (heroes & villains)
Becoming a superhero or supervillain grants a **superpower** drawn from a deep roster — super strength, flight, telepathy, invisibility, energy blasts, super speed, telekinesis, elemental control, shapeshifting, regeneration, technopathy, time manipulation, force fields, mind control, weather control, phasing, duplication, magnetism, sonic scream, laser vision. You unlock more as you rise through the stages, and you can **train your powers** for a chance at new ones. Powers improve your odds in showdowns and prison escapes.

### Living the hero life
On the Do tab, a hero can **patrol the city** (fame and risk), **train their powers**, and **build a secret lair**. Through events you'll gain a recurring **nemesis** — a named archvillain you duel again and again (defeat them enough times and you put them away for good), face **impossible choices** (a bus full of people or a child across town), get recruited by a **superhero team**, and risk having your **secret identity** exposed by a journalist.

### Living the villain life
A villain can **commit crime sprees** (money, but rising **Heat** — and high heat makes you far easier to catch), **recruit minions**, **lay low** to cool off, and **build a doomsday device**. Events let you choose your **schemes** (rob the gold reserve, corrupt a politician, hold the world hostage), manage a **henchman's loyalty** (or make a ruthless example of a traitor), get offered **redemption** by a hero (which you can accept, or fake before betraying them), and — at the very top, with a doomsday device in hand — make a **bid for world domination** that can either crown you Supreme Overlord of Earth or land you in the deepest cell ever built.

### Bribes, escapes & security escalation
Crime lords and supervillains aren't stuck once jailed — even for the biggest crimes or a life sentence. Each year inside, you can try to get out: **bribe a guard**, **stage a violent breakout**, or **use your powers**. Succeed and you walk free as a fugitive. Fail and you're transferred up the security ladder: **County Jail → Maximum Security → the Vault** (a Raft-style super-prison). Escape gets harder at each level, and the Vault is nearly unbeatable — but a powered villain can still try. Life sentences are escapable but markedly harder than fixed terms.
- **Mad Scientist** (key: Smarts) — reality-bending experiments with persistent, world-altering consequences (portals, clones, gravity, sentient pigeons).
  Garage Tinkerer → Fringe Researcher → Renegade Genius → Dimension Bender → Reality Architect
- **Scientist** (ladder): Lab Tech → Researcher → Senior Scientist → Lab Director → Nobel Laureate
- **Train Conductor** (ladder): Brakeman → Conductor → Senior Conductor → Yardmaster

### Part-time jobs (teens 13–17 and students)
Fast food, Grocery bagger, Paper route, Dog walker, Online freelance.

## Interactive choice events (pause the year, you decide)

Many plant a "seed" that pays off or backfires years later.

- **The lemonade stand** (child) — work ethic vs. play
- **A lost dog** (child) — keep / return / walk away (can get a pet)
- **A chance to cheat** (school) — cheat vs. study
- **The bully** (child) — stand up / tell / endure (seed: resilience)
- **The first real party** (teen) — sneak out / ask / stay home (seed: grounded)
- **Behind the wheel** (teen) — floor it vs. drive safe
- **A first crush** (teen) — confess / write / say nothing (seed: thick skin)
- **A dare at the mall** (teen) — shoplift vs. refuse
- **A risky offer** (adult) — startup leap vs. stay safe (seed: startup payoff in 5 yrs)
- **A friend in need** (adult) — lend $5k (seed: repaid in 3 yrs) vs. decline
- **A temptation** (married) — affair vs. walk away (seed: fallout)
- **The old house** (adult) — restore / sell / let rot
- **Push the limit?** (gym) — PR vs. safety
- **A business idea** (adult) — build a side hustle vs. let it go
- **You saw something** (adult) — report (seed: retaliation) vs. look away
- **Time to retire?** (62+) — retire vs. keep working
- **An old rift** (55+) — reconcile vs. let it lie

## Relationships

Parents, siblings, friends, romantic partners (Partner → Spouse → Ex-spouse → Late spouse), children. Each has a bond meter. Actions: spend time, date, marry, have a child, **casual fling**, view **past loves**, and **break up / divorce** (a Leave/Divorce button on any partner or spouse row — divorce splits assets and costs happiness).

### Sexual orientation
Around age 18 a coming-of-age prompt asks who the character is drawn to — opposite sex (straight), same sex (gay/lesbian), both (bi), or "rather not say" (left to chance). This determines the sex of everyone they date, hook up with, or marry. Most heirs default to straight, with a realistic minority gay or bi if never chosen.

### Partner history
Every spouse, ex-spouse, late spouse, and fling is recorded with the years it spanned and how many children came from it. View it from the People panel ("Past loves") — and a summary shows on the Self panel.

### Children
- Natural conception: odds fall with age, hard cap at 47.
- Fertility clinic: extends conception to 55 ($28k, can fail).
- Adoption: any adult age, can bring an older child ($18k).
- **Flings outside marriage** can produce a child (lower odds than a couple). These kids are full people, flagged as "born from a fling," and are inheritable heirs just like any other.
- Children age in the background and can be played as your heir after you die.

### Autonomy (offscreen lives)
Your adult children — and their children — live their own quiet lives each year: they get jobs, marry, have kids (your grandchildren), build a little wealth, sometimes divorce or pass away. This happens without logging, so when you inherit one they arrive with a life already lived. To keep the world from ballooning, only close kin (your children and grandchildren) run full autonomous lives, and the family tree is capped at ~140 people, pruning the oldest distant branches first. The player's direct line and any potential heirs are always protected.

### Heirs at death
When you die, you choose an heir from: your living children first, then grandchildren, then any living descendant further down the line. Because relatives now reproduce on their own, the bloodline rarely dead-ends.

## Growing up (0–18)

Childhood and adolescence now play out richly across the quarterly clock. Beyond the one-tap seasonal moments, you'll face a wide range of interactive choices — far more than just bullying:

- **Outings & family life** — trips to the mall (beg for a toy, hit the arcade, or wander off and get lost in a chained mini-adventure), getting **left behind** at a family gathering (tantrum, stay calm, or try to walk home), and shopping-trip temptations.
- **Birthday parties** — your own (be the center of attention, share the spotlight, or melt down over cake) and classmates' parties (make friends, bring the best gift, or hide by the snacks).
- **Friendships** — find a true **best friend** who sticks for life, weather a friend's **betrayal**, build bonds through the new sibling/friend "More…" actions, or keep people at arm's length.
- **School & talent** — talent shows, class-pet responsibility, summer camp, JV tryouts, science fairs, hallway fights that chain, pranks, and standing up for smaller kids.
- **Early lessons** — first allowance (save, splurge, or gift it), a bad sickness (rest or push through), and discovering a lifelong passion.

Many of these are **chained decisions**, and many **grant traits** (see below) that shape who your character becomes.

## Traits

Alongside the six main stats, characters collect **named traits** that nudge those stats every year. Good traits (green) like *Brilliant*, *Strikingly Attractive*, *Confident*, *Natural Athlete*, *Optimist*, and *Iron Constitution* push a stat upward over time; difficult traits (red) like *Anxious*, *Frail*, *Slow Learner*, *Hot-Tempered*, or *Pessimist* drag one down. They appear as chips on the Self page.

You acquire traits mostly through **childhood choices** — how you handle getting lost, a birthday party, a betrayal, a sickness — though life keeps shaping you as an adult too. Good traits are **heritable**: a Brilliant or Strikingly Attractive parent has a real chance of passing it to each child, so a bloodline can compound its gifts (or its struggles) across generations.

Don't like a trait you ended up with? Head to the **Health** tab. A **psychologist** can help you overcome a difficult trait over time, and a **personal coach** gives you a shot at training into a positive one.

## Names, marriage & prenups

You're no longer stuck with the names the game picks. When a **child is born** (or is adopted), a prompt lets you set their first name and choose their surname — yours, your partner's, or a hyphenated blend. Any partner, spouse, child, sibling, or friend can be **renamed** anytime through the "More…" button on their relationship row.

**Marriage** now opens a short set of decisions: whose **surname** changes (both keep their own, you take theirs, or they take yours), and whether to sign a **prenup**. A prenup protects each person's assets if the marriage later ends — in a divorce, a protected partner keeps far more of their fortune — though raising it can sting the relationship a little. The game flags when your partner appears to be the wealthier one, so you know when a prenup matters most.

## Moral choices

Throughout life, you'll hit **ethical dilemmas** with no single right answer: a lost wallet stuffed with cash, a chance to hide income from the taxman, taking credit for a junior colleague's idea, blowing the whistle on a dangerous employer (or taking a bribe to stay quiet), insurance fraud, and chances to give generously to a cause that moves you. Your choices quietly build a reputation — generous, honest, corrupt, ruthless — that colors the kind of person your character becomes.

## Life in prison

Getting locked up is no longer a dead end where you just wait out the clock. While inside, your normal life options are sealed off — but a whole **prison world** opens up. The Do tab becomes a prison menu: work out in the yard, use the library, and **build your reputation** on the block. Earn enough respect and you can run a **smuggling network** for cash, and at the very top **take over the entire prison** as the shot-caller — running the economy, corrupting guards, and dealing with rivals. Prison-specific events fire constantly: shakedowns, riots, education programs, parole hearings, and visits from family. Crime bosses and supervillains can still plan an escape from here, too. Everything resets when you're released or break out.

## Assets & businesses

There are now a dozen **businesses** to own, scaling from a humble food truck and laundromat up through restaurants, gyms, car washes, breweries, boutique hotels, tech startups, vineyards, and even a pro sports team — each generating yearly income. The **luxury** lineup has grown too: art collections, racehorses, private jets, country estates, watch collections, and more, alongside the classic islands, yachts, and supercars. Build a real portfolio and pass it down the bloodline.

## More superpowers

Powers are no longer only for heroes and villains. Anyone can stumble into abilities through rare life events — a freak accident, a mysterious mentor who unlocks hidden potential, or an ancient artifact humming with impossible energy. Heroes and villains can also keep training to expand their roster, drawing from an enlarged pool of twenty distinct powers.

## The influencer life

Building a social media following is now a real career path with its own deep menu on the Do tab, unlocking more options as you grow:

- **Grow your reach** — post content, make viral videos (high risk, high reward), and collab with other creators to swap audiences and make connections.
- **Cash in** — chase sponsorships, launch a merch line, start a podcast for steady fame, or stir up drama to spike followers at the cost of your reputation.
- **Hollywood comes calling** — once you're big enough, turn clout into screen deals: pursue **TV deals**, audition for **B-list movies** (indie and genre films), and from there climb toward a coveted **A-list movie role** that makes you a bona fide star. You can also **network with movie stars** to add A-listers to your circle.

But fame has a dark side. The party scene, cancellation, and burnout all lurk, and the pressure can push a creator toward substances.

## Addiction & recovery

Characters can now develop real, distinct addictions — **alcohol, cocaine, party drugs, painkillers, gambling, and nicotine** — each dragging on health and happiness in its own way (gambling bleeds money; painkillers and cocaine hit hardest). Addictions have **severity**: ignore one and it worsens, becoming harder to beat and, at its worst, life-threatening. You can carry more than one at a time.

Recovery is real too. The **Health** tab offers a dedicated **rehab program for each addiction** (the harder drugs cost more and have lower odds when severe), and life events like an "in too deep" intervention give you the chance to admit you need help — or spiral further. Beating an addiction restores health and happiness, a hard-won fresh start.

## The Career tab

There's now a dedicated **Career** tab in the bottom navigation, separate from the Do tab. It's the home for everything work-related:

- **Status at a glance** — your current title, salary, years in, and what the next promotion needs.
- **Find a career** — when unemployed, browse every path here, with requirements shown.
- **Advancement** — train your key stat to climb staged careers, or lay low in criminal ones.
- **On the job** — work harder for a bonus, ask for a raise, or quit.

The heart of it is **per-profession decisions** — choices unique to your specific career:

- **Fighters (UFC, boxing, wrestling)** — learn from a legend, call out a big name for a megafight payday, build hype with trash talk, or take **steroids/PEDs**. Juicing boosts your performance — but you risk failing a drug test. If your character is smart enough, they know how to time the cycle and beat the testing; if not, getting caught means suspension and disgrace.
- **Professor** — publish research, campaign to become **Dean**, mentor a star student, or take lucrative corporate consulting on the side.
- **Doctor** — specialize for a bigger salary, volunteer at a free clinic, or overprescribe for kickbacks (lucrative and very risky).
- **Lawyer / Judge** — take a high-profile case, make partner, or defend a guilty client for a fortune.
- **Tech** — push for promotion, build a side project that might get acquired, or job-hop for a raise.
- **Pop star / Musician / Movie star** — cross over into acting, launch a world tour, or embrace the rockstar lifestyle (with its addiction risks).
- **Politician** — run for higher office, take corporate donations that come with strings, or champion a popular bill.

Each profession's menu reflects its real temptations and ambitions, so the choices that define your character depend on the life you're living.

## Intimacy, pregnancy & affairs

When you're with a partner or spouse, the relationship panel now offers **Intimacy** instead of a plain "try for a child" button. You choose how the night goes:

- **Protected** — just enjoy it. A happiness boost, no risk.
- **Unprotected** — leave it to chance. Happiness, plus a real chance of conceiving.
- **Try for a baby** — actively trying, with the best odds.

Pregnancy is now a **state that plays out over time** rather than an instant birth. Once expecting, it shows on the Self page and carries to term about a year later, when you'll get to name the new arrival. If the player character is male (or his partner is the one who can carry), his partner becomes pregnant instead.

While expecting, the **Health tab** (or the relationship panel) lets you see a doctor: get **prenatal care** to give the baby a healthy start, or, within the early window, choose to **end the pregnancy** — a private, personal decision the game treats matter-of-factly. Older characters can still turn to the fertility clinic, and adoption remains open to anyone.

**Affairs** add real risk to commitment. Anyone in a relationship can choose to cheat. You might get away with it quietly — or get **caught**, devastating your partner's trust and forcing a choice between begging for forgiveness or leaving for the new lover. An affair can also lead to a **secret pregnancy**, at which point you can try to pass the child off as your partner's, come clean, or quietly see a doctor. Being sly or clever lowers your odds of getting caught.

## The villain's empire

Villainy is now a real operation you build, not just a job title:

- **Minions** — recruit a crew that grows over time. Minions aren't cosmetic: they add a substantial **passive income** every year (people working for you), reduce the chance you get caught, and a large enough crew can **break you out of prison** if you're ever locked up. You can train them to raise your crew's power, or send them to take the heat off you.
- **Crime spree & heists** — scores now **scale with your empire**. A small-timer pulls the $1M minimum, but a kingpin with hundreds of millions pulls heists worth tens or hundreds of millions. Use the general crime spree, or **plan a specific heist** — corner store up to a global crypto exchange — with bigger targets requiring a bigger, smarter crew.
- **Doomsday device** — now a multi-stage megaproject. Build the core, **arm it**, then **ransom the world** for a fortune (scaling with your wealth), or escalate to an outright **bid for world domination** that can crown you ruler of Earth — or land you in the deepest cell ever built.
- **Heat** — every crime raises your notoriety, which makes you easier to catch. Lay low yourself, or send your crew to muddy the trail.

New villain events deepen the life further: a street gang offering to join you, mutinies in your ranks, doomsday-device tests, and henchman betrayals — each with choices that shape the kind of crime lord you become. All of it shows on your Self page: crew size, crew power, doomsday status, and heat.

## The hero's calling

The superhero life is now a full operation, mirroring the villain's depth:

- **Patrol the city** — stop crimes, build your **reputation**, and rack up a running tally of **lives saved**. Your success scales with your powers, athleticism, and gear, and a wounded hero can still pull through if they're well-equipped.
- **Gear & base** — upgrade from a humble Workshop all the way to an Orbital HQ. Better gear makes your heroics stronger and your injuries rarer.
- **Sidekick** — take on a partner, train them up, and fight as a duo (just be ready when they're put in danger).
- **Fund the mission** — turn your fame and reputation into endorsements and merch income, so heroism can actually pay.
- **Hunt your nemesis** — take the fight to your archenemy proactively rather than only reacting to their schemes.

New hero events add real moral and emotional weight: a city-leveling **disaster** where hundreds need saving, a **corruption** offer testing whether you can be bought, your **sidekick taken hostage**, and the quiet toll of **burnout** after years under the cape. Your reputation, lives saved, gear level, sidekick, and nemesis all show on the Self page.

## Bribing your way out

Bribery is no longer one-size-fits-all. When locked up, you can offer a **small, medium, or large** bribe — from thousands up into the millions, scaling with the prison's security. The bigger the envelope you can afford, the better your odds of walking free: a small bribe is a coin-flip, but a large one usually works. Wealthy characters can even attempt to buy their way out of the Vault. Being sly or lucky, or having a capable crew, sweetens the odds further.

## A life remembered

When a character dies, the memorial screen now shows a full **recap of their life** before you choose an heir: the milestones they reached, the achievements they earned (championships, fortunes, lives saved or ruined, crews commanded, prizes won), the crimes on their record, their struggles, who their children were, and a list of the **defining moments** from their story. It's a fitting send-off that captures who they really were. The heir-selection list is now fully **scrollable**, so even a character with a houseful of children lets you scroll through and pick any of them to continue the bloodline.

## Crime lord & mad scientist

Two more careers got the deep-operation treatment in the Career tab:

**Organized Crime** is now an empire you run. Expand your **territory** block by block in turf wars, open **rackets** (gambling dens, drug distribution, money laundering, arms trafficking, construction shakedowns) that each pay yearly, collect **protection money**, put cops and judges on the payroll to lower your heat, and order hits on rivals. Your turf and rackets generate serious passive income on top of your scores.

**Mad Scientist** can run dangerous **experiments** with reality-bending world consequences, sell cutting-edge tech to shadowy buyers, build a loyal artificial creation, and even chase a **Nobel Prize** to turn mad genius into celebrated legitimacy.

## More careers with real choices

Another wave of careers now has its own decisions in the Career tab:

**Pro athletes (basketball, football, soccer)** — hit the training facility, push for a max contract, chase a signature shoe deal, become the face of the league, play through the pain, or quietly take performance enhancers (with the same smarts-hides-it risk as the fighters).

**Police officer** — volunteer for dangerous duty, climb the ranks from Officer to Detective, Sergeant, Lieutenant and Chief, serve the community to build trust, or take dirty money and risk Internal Affairs.

**Soldier** — volunteer for tours of duty, rise from Private all the way to General, try out for special forces, or transition home to civilian life.

**Firefighter** — run into burning buildings to save lives, make Captain, pull off dramatic rescues, or teach fire safety in the community.

**Startup founder** — raise venture capital, pivot the company, crunch toward deadlines, or sell the company in a blockbuster exit that sets you up for life.

**Astronaut** — volunteer for risky missions, train for a history-making Moon or Mars landing, or conduct groundbreaking orbital research.

## Creative careers with real choices

The glamorous creative paths now have their own decisions in the Career tab:

**Film director** — take a passion project for critical acclaim, direct a big-budget blockbuster, campaign for an Oscar, or mentor a rising filmmaker.

**Esports pro** — grind ranked into the night, compete in the world championship for prize money and glory, stream to build a fanbase, or move into coaching to extend your career.

**Model** — walk for a top fashion house, land a brand ambassador deal, launch your own beauty line, or prioritize real health over industry pressure.

**Author** — write your magnum opus, sell the film rights to Hollywood, chase a literary prize, or churn out a commercial bestseller that sells big while critics sniff.

**Actor** — take a serious dramatic role, chase a franchise blockbuster, campaign for an Oscar, or cross over into directing.

**YouTuber** — make viral videos, sign brand sponsorships, sell channel merch, or survive a controversy.

**Restaurateur** — chase a Michelin star, open new locations to grow your empire, become a celebrity chef with a TV show, or publish a cookbook.

**Secret agent** — take deep-cover missions, angle for promotion up to Director, go private for lucrative contracts, or risk everything by going rogue.

## A richer childhood, school & college

The early years and the choices that shape a person are much deeper now. Growing up, your character faces a wide range of formative moments: a science fair, free music lessons, befriending (or teasing) the new kid, owning up to a broken vase or letting a sibling take the blame, the championship game's final play, what to do with saved-up allowance, the school play and stage fright, and the cruel temptation to join in bullying. Many of these quietly shape traits and reputation that follow the character for life.

The teen years add a part-time job dilemma, the driving test, **prom night** (with a chained "big ask" if you go for your crush), and college applications. And college itself is now a real chapter: frat parties versus midterms, choosing a major (money, passion, or the easy path), landing a competitive internship, an academic-ethics test, and the chance to study abroad and come back changed.

## More decisions in adult life

The pop-up dilemmas you meet as an adult now come in far greater variety, and several branch into follow-up choices. New ones include a startup founder's pitch, a hot crypto tip, a friend asking for a loan, a contested inheritance, a glittering charity gala, a stranger stranded on the roadside, an unexpected windfall, a young person seeking a mentor, a business partnership that plays out over time, and a property to flip or rent. Each is a small fork in the road — invest or play it safe, help or look away, chase the money or follow your conscience — and the consequences ripple through your character's wealth, relationships, and reputation.

## Random street encounters

Life throws surprises at your character, and who they are shapes how those moments play out.

The classic **mugging** is now much richer. When you fight back, your odds factor in not just athleticism and toughness but your **profession** — fighters, soldiers, secret agents, cops, firefighters, and even superheroes and villains all bring their training to the alley — plus any superpowers. And once you've won, your options depend on who you are: anyone can make a citizen's arrest or let the mugger flee, a **cop or hero** can try to show them a better path, and a **villain or crime lord** can rob the mugger right back or even **recruit them into their crew**. There's also a new option to talk your way out if you're charming or clever.

Beyond muggings, a whole variety of encounters can find your character on the street — and many key off your stats:

- If you're **strikingly good-looking**, a talent scout might offer you a shot at a movie or modeling career, or an admirer might approach you at a café.
- If you're **exceptionally athletic**, a recruiter might invite you to try out for a pro team or combat sports.
- If you're **brilliant**, a professor might steer you toward a research career or an academic olympiad.
- A street musician might pull you into a song, a fortune teller might read your future, a hustler might run a shell game, a tourist might need directions, you might spot a dropped wallet, get swept into a protest, be invited into a casino's high-roller room, be offered a dubious "free sample," or get pitched a too-good-to-be-true business.

Each is a small fork with real consequences — some lead to chained follow-ups, and the outcomes ripple through your money, health, looks, joy, reputation, and relationships. The path you're on changes which doors open.

## Everyday careers with real choices

The down-to-earth professions a lot of lives are built on now have their own decisions too:

**Nurse** — specialize for better pay, go above and beyond for a patient, pick up grueling overtime, or train toward nurse practitioner. **Teacher** — inspire a struggling student, earn a master's for a raise, climb toward principal, or tutor over the summer. **Engineer** — file a patent, lead a flagship project, move into management, or build a side project that might sell. **Accountant** — become a CPA, start your own practice, specialize in wealthy clients, or risk everything cooking the books. **Real estate agent** — chase luxury listings, build a personal brand, invest in rentals, or open your own brokerage. **Airline pilot** — upgrade to captain, fly long-haul, handle an in-flight emergency, or become a flight instructor. **Veterinarian** — open a clinic, pull off a miracle surgery, specialize in exotic animals, or volunteer at a shelter. Financial analysts, corporate workers, and chefs share fitting decision sets too.

## Even more random encounters

The world keeps surprising your character: an open-mic challenge, a class reunion where your life so far is on display, being caught up in a robbery as a bystander, a letter from a lawyer about a stranger's estate, a street portrait, a feat-of-strength challenge at the gym, a spontaneous weekend getaway, getting filmed out of context, and a street preacher's question about whether you're happy. Each keys off your stats and history in its own way.

## The twilight years

The end of a long life now has as much texture as the beginning. As your character ages into their sixties and beyond, they face a chapter of their own: writing a **will** (split it evenly, favor one child, or leave it all to charity), being a **grandparent** (doting, wisdom-keeper, or distant), writing a **memoir**, tackling a retirement **bucket list** (travel the world, a wild new hobby, reconnecting with old friends), navigating **health decline** with grace or defiance, receiving a **lifetime achievement award**, making a **final reconciliation** before it's too late, and **passing the torch** of everything they built to an heir or protégé. These choices shape the legacy that appears in the life-recap when they pass.

## Deeper relationships

The people in your character's life now drive far more of the story, with events spanning friendship, marriage, in-laws, and family drama.

**Friendships** evolve: a falling-out you can mend or let die, a friend's big success that tests your generosity, a friend who's a bad influence, and a voice from the past reaching out after decades. Bonds rise and fall with how you treat people, and friendships can quietly end if neglected.

**Marriage** is more than a status now. Couples hit rough patches you can work through with counseling or a second honeymoon (or let fester), face clashes when one spouse's career pulls against the other's, and reach milestone anniversaries where you can renew your vows. A strong, lasting marriage becomes part of your legacy.

**In-laws** enter the picture when you marry: a first big family dinner where you can charm them, be yourself, or clash — and later, a meddling in-law you'll have to set boundaries with, win over, or leave to your spouse. They become real people in your relationships list.

**Family drama** runs deep: grown-up sibling rivalry, a long-buried family secret surfacing, an aging parent who needs care (move them in, pay for care, share the load with siblings, or carry the guilt of saying no), a rebellious child you can come down hard on or try to understand, and the holiday family reunion where you can keep the peace, stir the pot, or stay out of it.

Many of these choices shape bonds that follow you for life — and the warmest ones (a lifelong love, being the heart that held the family together, caring for an aging parent) are remembered in your life-recap when you pass.

## Superpowers in everyday life

Superpowers are no longer just for heroes and villains. If anyone acquires a power — through a freak accident, a mentor, or a mysterious artifact — it now gives a real, often decisive edge in whatever career fits it:

- **Telepathy or mind control** make a **lawyer** nearly unbeatable (you can literally read the jury), and help politicians, agents, salespeople, doctors, and negotiators of every kind.
- **Super strength, super speed, or regeneration** turn a **pro athlete or fighter** into an unstoppable champion — a powered fighter essentially never loses, dominates the championship, and plays far past a normal retirement age.
- **Technopathy** lets a **tech worker or founder** speak directly to machines and ship the impossible. **Telekinesis** gives surgeons, engineers, and mechanics inhuman precision.
- **Flight** helps pilots and first responders, **shapeshifting** makes a flawless actor or spy, **invisibility** is a gift for agents, and many more pairings besides.

When your power matches your job, you earn more, get promoted faster, shrug off the dangers of risky work, and unlock a special **"use your power on the job"** action in the Career tab for a big, decisive win — plus profession-specific power moves like reading the jury's minds as a lawyer or dominating the championship as a powered athlete. Your Self page flags when a power is boosting your current career, so you'll know to lean into it.

## Fame and your social media following

Joining social media no longer starts everyone at zero. Your built-in audience now depends on **who you already are** — your fame and the public profile of your career.

A pro athlete, movie star, or musician who joins social media instantly arrives with a massive following — often **hundreds of thousands to millions** of followers, because the world already knows them. A lawyer or doctor draws a **modest** audience (tens of thousands), reflecting a respected-but-not-famous profile. A teacher, nurse, or accountant starts small and has to build from there, just like in real life. Championships, being the face of your league, good looks, and natural charm all add to the head start.

It works the other way too: if you're already on social media and then **break into a high-profile career** — say a teacher who gets discovered and turns pro — your following surges to match your new fame. This ties directly into the influencer track, so a famous athlete can pivot into sponsorships, merch, and even Hollywood crossover deals far faster than an unknown ever could.

## Prison: a criminal's proving ground

For criminals, crime lords, and villains, prison is no longer dead time — it's a chapter that can forge a more dangerous boss than the one who walked in.

Inside, life is violent and full of choices: a shank in the dark you have to survive, a witness against your organization that the boss outside wants silenced, a guard you can bribe, blackmail, or recruit, a brutal gang war you can win or broker peace in, time in solitary that hardens or breaks you, and a legendary aging kingpin who might take you on as a protégé — teaching you the contacts and cold arithmetic of power. You can settle scores, climb from nobody to the shot-caller who runs the entire block, and run rackets that pay tribute every quarter.

Your **crew comes with you**: a minion or your henchman can get sent up on a separate charge and join you inside, letting the operation keep running from behind bars. You can recruit sharp inmates who'll work for you once they're released, and groom a hardened lieutenant from the cells.

And crucially, a criminal **comes out worse — by which they mean better**. Serve your time as a crime boss and you walk out tougher (boosted strength and street smarts), more feared (a reputation that precedes you), and more powerful in the underworld: bigger crew strength, more territory, a fresh enforcer waiting, and chilling new traits like *tough*, *sly*, and *brave* forged in the cellblock. Making your bones inside earns a fearsome reputation that follows you onto the streets — and into your life-recap. Non-criminals, by contrast, can use the time to rehabilitate and start fresh, or come out hardened by what they endured.

## Retirement

Retirement is now a real, distinct chapter of life rather than just quitting work. Once your character is 60+ with a few years of work behind them, the Career tab offers a proper **Retire** option, and your status changes to "Retired."

When you retire you receive a **pension** based on your peak salary and how long you worked — a 40-year career earns a far better pension than a 10-year one. On top of that, **Social Security** begins at 65 (you can claim it early at 62 for a reduced amount, or wait for the full benefit), scaling with your years of work. Together these give retirees a real income without a job, so a long, well-paid career sets you up to live comfortably.

Retirement has its own life, too. Through the Career tab you can take an easygoing **encore part-time job**, do lucrative **consulting** in your old field, **volunteer**, or simply enjoy a **hobby** that lifts your health and spirits. Dedicated events capture the rhythm of these years: the restlessness of suddenly having nowhere to be, the decision of when to claim benefits, and **downsizing** the family home (sell for cash, keep it for the grandkids, or move somewhere sunny). And if retirement doesn't suit you, you can always come out of it and find a new job. A comfortable, well-traveled, or paradise retirement is remembered in your life-recap.

## The Homelander — the bloodline's true goal

Somewhere in this world lives **Homelander**: an all-powerful narcissist who possesses every one of the game's **50 superpowers** and uses them with breathtaking cruelty. He mostly ignores ordinary people — with billions alive, your family is beneath his notice — but every so often (about 3% of the time you advance) a news blurb reports his latest atrocity: incinerating protesters, slicing a bridge or a passenger jet in half, sinking a submarine that "disrespected" him. He simply exists, a god-tyrant in the background of every life you play.

Ending him is the work of dozens of generations. **A single character can only ever acquire 2 new powers in their lifetime** — some manifest automatically as they grow, others come from rare events (a freak accident, a mysterious mentor, an ancient artifact, a meditation retreat). But **children inherit the combined powers of both parents**, so the bloodline accumulates abilities down the generations. Pair with **other powered partners** and the line grows stronger faster — a discovery that rewards the attentive player.

**Power synthesis:** certain pairs combine. If your bloodline holds two specific powers (say Telepathy and Empathy), the next heir is simply *born* with a third (Dream Walking) — ten such combinations exist, quietly speeding the journey. Reaching all 50 powers realistically takes **25 to 50 generations**. This is the long game.

### The Vault

Powers alone aren't enough. Across the generations your descendants must also fill **the Vault** — 30 items, each secured at the absolute peak of a specific career. **15 are required** to beat Homelander; the other 15 are optional, improving the fight's odds and richness. Reaching a career's summit triggers a **choice**: store the item for a bloodline that will never know your sacrifice, or take a tempting reward instead (a fortune, a Nobel, an Oscar, a championship purse). Every career can yield one — from President (The Order) and Mad Scientist (The Virus) to a 30-year Truck Driver or Electrician whose humble mastery hides something a President can't provide. A new **Media Mogul** career (Intern Reporter → Network President) yields The Broadcast.

You can review your progress and the full vault any time in the **Self tab** — powers collected (X/50), required vault items (X/15), and an "Open the vault" view listing every item, where it's earned, and what it does.

**No biological heir?** A bloodline doesn't have to end. A **Mad Scientist** can research a forbidden **power transfusion** (a career choice), and once unlocked, transfuse their powers into an **adopted** heir — passing on roughly **two-thirds** of their powers (randomly chosen, never all of them). The adopted heir then carries the bloodline's gifts forward to their own children normally, and still keeps their own two-power lifetime allowance on top. It's the bloodline's safety valve for a generation that adopts rather than gives birth.

### The Homelander tab

Once your bloodline is on the path (any character with a power, or simply later generations), a new **☄️ Homelander tab** appears in the bottom bar. Everything endgame-related lives here: your powers and vault progress, your **bloodline awareness** level (how closely Vought and Homelander are watching you — Dormant → Noticed → Watched → HUNTED), Vought's evolving PR news blurbs, the sub-boss fights, the sycophant options, and the fight itself.

**Vought's lieutenants (sub-bosses).** Three milestone fights gate the road to Homelander, each unlocking at a power threshold: **The Deep** (10 powers), **Translucent** (20), and **Firecracker** (30). None of them necessarily kills you — they're dangerous but flawed. Attempt one before its threshold and you'll survive but suffer (bruised ribs, raised awareness, footage of you losing). Attempt one once you're ready and each has its own mechanic: the Deep is a coward you can fight or *talk to* (hearing him out gives a better reward — his psychological file on Homelander); Translucent is a puzzle (only certain powers like phasing, electrokinesis, or a tuned sonic scream pierce his diamond skin); Firecracker is a simultaneous physical *and* media battle. Each defeat hands you a vault item and changes the news blurbs — Vought's PR language visibly strains and cracks as you take down its enforcers. Defeating all three also weakens Homelander going into the final fight.

**The sycophant options.** You can publicly flatter Homelander — fan mail, donations, online defense. This lowers your awareness (a fawning bloodline looks harmless) and very rarely triggers easter-egg responses from the sub-bosses. Best of all, if your bloodline has played the sycophant enough, Homelander *underestimates* you when the real fight comes.

### The fight, and the cycle

**The premature challenge.** From the Homelander tab you can challenge him before you're ready — and you will die, every time. But it's never a dead end: the fight plays out in six escalating tiers based on how many powers you hold, each a different story. Under 9 powers, Homelander doesn't even come — *Vought* quietly handles you. Higher up, you last eight seconds, then make him take a single step back, then draw first blood, then have him for forty seconds, and finally — at 45-49 powers — lose by the one capability you never gathered. Each tier leaves a world-history mark and teaches the next heir how close the bloodline is.

**The full confrontation** unlocks only with **all 50 powers AND all 15 required vault items**. It's a multi-phase set piece: his arrival (which changes based on your awareness level, sycophant history, and whether you cleared all three sub-bosses), a series of power-exchange beats drawn from your actual abilities, then the vault activating piece by piece — each item naming its contribution. Then a **crisis choice** decides how it ends, and which ending you can pick depends on which vault items you hold:

- **End it** (needs Super Strength + The Gloves + Invulnerability + Regeneration) — you kill him. The most complete victory; you survive but barely; 20 powers burn out.
- **Imprison him** (needs The Precedent + The Mandate + The Order) — caged alive; only 15 powers burn; he escapes in ~12 years.
- **Let the world see** (needs The Broadcast + Exposé Reel + Documentary + Manifesto) — exposed on live global broadcast; only 10 powers burn; he retreats, weakened.
- **The Device** (needs The Device + The Order) — the doomsday option. Complete but pyrrhic: **your character dies**, all 50 powers reset, the bloodline starts again from near-nothing.

After any victory, the **Dimensional Pocket** power (if held) safeguards the vault; otherwise much of it scatters. Within roughly 10-15 years **a new Homelander rises**, and the bloodline rebuilds. (There's also a tiny 0.05% chance each time you advance that Homelander carelessly kills your current character — another reason to keep the line going.) Meanwhile every other path stays open: careers, family, wealth, crime, retirement, legacy. The Homelander arc is the long game running quietly beneath an ordinary, extraordinary life.

## Starting a new life

When you begin, you can set your character's first and last name, **type your own birth city and country** (e.g. "Los Angeles, United States"), reroll traits/origin, flip the sex, or hit 🎲 Random city. The city you choose becomes your hometown.

## College life

While enrolled at **University** (a 4-year degree), two new worlds open up on the Do tab:

- **Greek life** — pledge one of three fraternities (Omega Chi, Sigma Tau, Kappa Rho) or three sororities (Delta Nu, Pi Beta, Theta Kai), each with its own vibe and a stat perk (connections, social life, or smarts).
- **College sports** — walk onto the basketball, football, or track team (needs athletic ability). On graduation, a basketball/football standout can be **drafted into the NBA/NFL**, and a top track athlete can **qualify for the Olympics** and medal — launching fame and fortune.

## Fight careers (UFC & WWE)

UFC and WWE run on a **fight-camp cadence** tied to the quarterly clock. Roughly twice a year a fight comes up and you choose how to prepare — train hard, study your opponent, cut weight and rest, or sell the fight on the mic. Then the fight happens:

- **UFC** is real competition: wins depend on your athletic skill and prep, you build a win/loss record, and a strong streak earns a **title shot**. Being "over" (famous and exciting) lands headline bonuses on top of the belt — sometimes being a draw pays more than holding the belt.
- **WWE** is scripted: outcomes are booked toward whoever's most **over** with the crowd, so fame drives everything. Win the crowd and you'll be booked as **WWE Champion**.

Fighting is dangerous — losses and bumps cost health, and a career can end in injury or retirement around age 40.

## More careers

- **Delivery Driver** — an Amazon-style gig with peak-season bonuses and a sore back.
- **Professor → Dean** — climb the academic ladder from Adjunct to Department Dean. In the university setting, an adult graduate student may express interest (strictly 18+).
- **AI Startup → IPO → CEO** — found a company, raise a Seed round and Series A, scale to a Unicorn, and take it public in a blockbuster **IPO** for a life-changing windfall.

## How time works

Each button press advances **one quarter (3 months)** — Winter, Spring, Summer, Autumn. The log window **clears each press** and shows only what happened that season under a season header. Quarters can have their own small events and interactive choices. The **4th press of the year ("End of Year")** runs the full life simulation — job progress, stat changes, big events — and your character ages a year. So roughly four taps make a year.

### Chained decisions
Some choices trigger an **immediate follow-up**. Shove someone at a bar and they may swing back — and whether you win the fight depends on your athletic stat, combat careers (UFC/boxer/WWE), powers, and traits. A mugging can branch into a struggle that plays out over several linked decisions.

### Life history
The window only shows the current season, so your full past lives on the **Life history** page (open the Self tab → 📜 Life history). It's grouped by age and season. There's also a **World history** page showing the persistent deeds your bloodline has left on the world.

## Money & assets

- **Investment portfolio:** Low / Medium / High risk tiers, each with its own return profile each year.
- **Businesses:** Online Casino, Restaurant, Rental Property (pay yearly income).
- **Houses (tiered):** tiny apartment → starter home → family house → big suburban → estate → mansion. Bigger houses have more bedrooms; buying a new one sells the old one toward it. A big house (or high fame) invites **family members to ask to move in**.
- **Other assets:** cars (depreciate), luxury (island, yacht, helicopter, supercar, RV, jewelry).
- **Social media:** join, post, grow followers into brand deals.
- Everything liquidates into your **estate** and passes to heirs at death.

## Country & power

From the Do tab, adults can **emigrate** to another country (changing citizenship) and **seek to rule** a nation — odds rise with fame, wealth, looks, and the Charming trait. If you take power you become President / Prime Minister / Supreme Leader / Chancellor, can **pass laws** (each a persistent world event), and must **hold onto power** against elections and coups — lose and you may even be imprisoned.

## Persistent world state

Some deeds change the world and are remembered across generations: a superhero saving the city, a villain holding it hostage, a mad scientist opening a portal, a ruler passing a sweeping law. These accumulate in the game's world history, so your bloodline lives in a world shaped by its ancestors.

## Health

Insurance (premium scales with age), doctor visits, therapy, rehab, cosmetic surgery, meditation. Medical emergencies are survivable but ruinous without insurance. Tracks stress, addiction, and chronic conditions.

## Crime & consequences

Two paths. **Petty crime** (Do menu) can pay off or land you in prison with a record. The **Organized Crime career** is a full ladder from street hustler to kingpin with escalating bust risk, prison sentences that scale with rank, possible **life sentences**, **parole**, and **appeals** that can overturn a conviction years later. A criminal record shows on the Self panel and in the obituary.

---

## Generational system

When you die, choose a living child as your heir and continue the bloodline. Estate splits among heirs. The Tree view shows every life across every generation. If no heirs survive, the bloodline ends.
